Problem and project aim

Problem
Meaningful change
SuperFriend needed to measure whether changes in ITW variables over time were real and meaningful.
Why I was required
External expertise
The project needed an intern to help develop the methodology and build internal capability.
Aim
Repeatable method
Create an analysis approach SuperFriend could apply independently to future ITW survey waves.

Project focus: Develop a repeatable time-series style methodology to track psychological distress, burnout, workplace domains and psychosocial hazards across quarterly ITW waves in 2024 and 2025.

Important limitation: The data is cross-sectional, not longitudinal. This means the analysis tracks population-level change across seasonal waves, but does not follow the same individual workers over time.

My role and methods

From raw survey data to decision-ready insights

My role was to build a repeatable analysis workflow that could help SuperFriend track workforce mental health changes across repeated ITW survey waves.

Step 1

Method note: Because the data is repeated cross-sectional rather than longitudinal, the analysis tracks population-level change across waves, not the same workers over time.

Statistical tests used

Quarter differences
Pairwise t-tests
Compared quarters to identify where distress, burnout, domains and hazards were meaningfully different.
Why: shows which quarters changed, not just whether a trend exists.
Multiple testing
Bonferroni correction
Adjusted p-values because many quarter comparisons were tested.
Why: reduces the chance of false significant results.
Trend over time
Linear regression
Tested whether outcomes increased or decreased across quarterly waves.
Why: confirms whether visual trends are statistically supported.
High-risk group
Chi-square + trend test
Tested whether the proportion of workers with K6 ≥ 13 changed across quarters.
Why: focuses on clinically meaningful distress risk.
Economic links
Correlation tests
Checked relationships between financial distress, psychological distress, ABS LCI and RBA rates.
Why: connects worker wellbeing with economic pressure.
Group differences
Interaction regression
Compared whether blue-collar and white-collar workers responded differently to interest-rate pressure.
Why: shows whether financial pressure affects worker groups differently.

Overall purpose: These tests helped separate meaningful workforce signals from normal quarterly variation and explain why some groups may need more targeted support.

Final takeaways

Project objective: Develop a repeatable approach to identify meaningful workforce mental health and financial distress signals across ITW survey waves, and translate those signals into practical workplace action.

When to act

Who to support
Risk is uneven

What to consider
Economic context matters
