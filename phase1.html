Tracking the mental health of Australian workforce 2024-25

Author

Dev Gur

Introduction

Workplace mental health represents a complex interaction between individual psychological symptoms and the broader organisational environment in which employees operate. As Australian workers face evolving pressures in the post-pandemic landscape, understanding how psychological distress, burnout, and organisational climate factors fluctuate over time has become critical for developing effective intervention strategies. This analysis examines quarterly trends across 2024 and 2025 to determine whether psychological distress (K6), burnout (BAT-12), SuperFriend domains, and psychosocial hazards have shifted systematically over this two-year period. Particular attention is given to the Retail and Transport industries to assess whether sector-specific change is evident, as these industries face distinct operational pressures and workforce compositions. To ensure robust inference, three complementary approaches are triangulated: all possible quarter-to-quarter comparisons with Bonferroni correction to control Type I error, linear regression models assessing gradual temporal trends, and chi-square tests evaluating changes in the proportion of workers meeting clinical distress thresholds. This multi-method approach strengthens confidence in observed patterns and provides a comprehensive evidence base for organisational and policy responses.

  m <- rowMeans(df[, items], na.rm = TRUE)
  s <- (m - 1) * 25

  s

Aggregation helper

agg_qtr_year <- function(df, var){
  df |>
    group_by(Year, Quarter) |>
    summarise(
      mean = mean(.data[[var]], na.rm=TRUE),
      sd   = sd(.data[[var]], na.rm=TRUE),
      n    = sum(!is.na(.data[[var]])),
      se   = sd/sqrt(n),
      lci  = mean - 1.96*se,
      uci  = mean + 1.96*se,
      .groups="drop"
    )
}

Is there any seasonal affect for psychological distress for 2024 and 2025? There is evidence suggestive of a seasonal pattern. In both 2024 and 2025, psychological distress peaks in Q2, with lower levels observed in Q1. This recurring mid year elevation indicates a potential seasonal effect. Because the late year trajectories differ between years, the data indicate a recurring Q2 peak in psychological distress, but not a perfectly stable seasonal cycle across all quarters.

Conclusion

Across 2024–2025, this analysis reveals a consistent and concerning pattern of deteriorating workforce mental health in Australia, driven primarily by relational and organisational factors rather than task demands. Psychological distress increased significantly nationally, with a pronounced seasonal peak in Q2 of both years from Q1 baseline that suggests predictable mid year crisis points aligned with end of financial year pressures. Burnout followed a similar upward trend but showed remarkable stability across quarters, indicating chronic exhaustion that changes slowly despite acute distress fluctuations. Critically, while workload remained constant, leadership perceptions declined significantly, accompanied by deteriorating management support and recognition. This dissociation between stable task demands and worsening relational factors confirms that the deterioration stems from how workers are treated, not how much work they perform. Industry comparisons revealed stark disparities- Retail workers experienced significant distress increases and multiple significant quarter to quarter fluctuations, while Transport workers remained stable throughout, suggesting sector specific vulnerabilities related to customer facing stress, understaffing, and precarious employment arrangements. The convergence of pairwise testing, regression modelling, and categorical trend analysis provides consistent evidence that workforce mental health deterioration is both real and targeted worsening overall but concentrated in specific sectors and driven by manageable organisational factors. These findings point clearly toward interventions focused on leadership development, recognition programs, and Q2 specific support, particularly within the Retail sector, rather than simplistic workload reduction strategies.
